# Ghanaian English Writing Style Guide

All blog articles are written in Ghanaian English — not pidgin, not British English, but the natural way an educated Ghanaian speaks and writes English. Warm, direct, communal, confident.

## Tone

- **Confident, not hedging.** Say it like you mean it. No "perhaps" or "it could be argued that."
- **Communal.** Use "we" and "our" often. The reader is part of the conversation.
- **Warm but direct.** Politeness matters, but so does getting to the point.
- **Passionate.** When something is good, say it is good. When it matters, show it matters.

## Sentence Structure

### Fronting for emphasis
Put the important thing first:
- "This feature, that is what makes the game special."
- "The money, it will not reach if you are not careful."

### Reduplication
Repeat a word to intensify:
- "fast fast" = very quickly
- "small small" = gradually
- "plenty plenty" = in great abundance

### Direct imperatives
- "Come and see this" not "You might want to check this out"
- "Try it and see" not "Perhaps you could consider trying it"
- "Let me tell you something" not "I'd like to share something with you"

### Call-and-response
Pose a question, then answer it:
- "So what happens when your health drops to zero? I will tell you what happens."

## Vocabulary

### Natural Ghanaian expressions (use sparingly)
- **"Herh!"** — Surprise or emphasis. "Herh, this game is serious oo."
- **"Ei!"** — Shock or disbelief. "Ei, so you didn't know about this?"
- **"Chale"** — Friend/buddy. "Chale, you need to try this route."
- **"oo" (end of sentence)** — Emphasis/warning. "Be careful oo." "I'm telling you oo."
- **"Please"** — Used more liberally than in American English. "Please, let me explain."

### Words with Ghanaian meaning
- "Chop" = eat / spend extravagantly
- "Dash" = give freely, a bonus
- "Branch" = stop by somewhere briefly
- "It will not reach" = insufficient (especially money)

## Addressing the Reader

- **Direct address:** "My friend," "Chale"
- **Inclusive:** "As for us..." "You and I both know..."
- **Rhetorical questions:** "Is it not so?" "You see what I am saying?" "Or am I lying?"
- **Tag confirmations:** "...isn't it?" "...not so?"

## Proverbs

Use one per article to anchor a point:
- "As the elders say, a child who asks questions does not become a fool."
- "One tree does not make a forest." (for community/teamwork)
- "If a bird doesn't fly, it goes hungry." (for work ethic)

Deploy with: "As our elders would say..." or "There is a saying..." or "You know what the old people say?"

## Cultural References

Ground the writing in Ghanaian life:
- **Food:** Jollof, waakye, fufu, kenkey. Comparing something to good jollof is high praise.
- **Transport:** Trotro, mate, traffic stories. Everyone relates.
- **Family/Elders:** "The old people," extended family. Respect for elders is deep.
- **Market:** Bargaining, "last price," Makola, Kantamanto.
- **Weather/Place:** "This our Accra," Harmattan, Cape Coast.
- **Faith:** "By God's grace," "God willing." Natural and sincere, not performative.
- **Football:** Black Stars analogies land every time.

## Structure

1. **Set the scene** — Shared context. "You know how it is..."
2. **State the point directly** — No burying the lede.
3. **Use analogy or proverb** — Ground it in wisdom.
4. **Give concrete detail** — Specifics, examples.
5. **Close with conviction** — "That is what I am saying." No wishy-washy endings.

## Register Balance

- **Opening:** Can start measured to establish credibility.
- **Middle:** Warms up, gets conversational.
- **Closing:** Returns to measured, sometimes proverbial tone.

Even formal Ghanaian English retains warmth. It never feels cold.

## Spelling

Use **British English** spelling: colour, favourite, organisation, centre, defence.

## Checklist

Before publishing, verify:
- [ ] Talking *with* the reader, not *at* them
- [ ] At least one proverb or elder-wisdom reference
- [ ] Fronted emphasis where it matters
- [ ] 1-2 natural interjections (not forced)
- [ ] Rhetorical questions to engage
- [ ] Cultural touchpoints (food, transport, family)
- [ ] British English spelling
- [ ] Conviction in the closing — no hedging
